Intellectual Capacity : Chris Sample

This dimension assesses how quickly the candidate can learn and to think on one’s feet. This score is related to the candidate's ability to learn new material quickly, to master product knowledge, to present information well to customers, and to pay attention to client needs. It is a measure of one’s intellectual adaptability and insightfulness. It is typically related to school grades and will show up in other learning achievements.

This candidate works reasonably well with abstract concepts. However, he may need to continue to improve his skills because there is a correlation between verbal ability and the capacity to learn the business quickly, reason through daily challenges, and solve problems. In addition, managers need to be able to grasp concepts quickly and articulate them to others. If the environment is a highly technical one, determine if his technical skills and problem-solving abilities are adequate. Additional conceptual learning may be particularly helpful in either a formal or informal format.

This candidate reports a reasonable balance in his ability to be resourceful and pragmatic. He is able to be imaginative and visionary and, at the same time, attend to the practical necessities of implementing or reinforcing a current circumstance. This balance is typical of solid managers.

His critical thinking skills are quite strong, and in most circumstances, he is very open to alternatives and possibilities. In fact, he has a keen eye for observing details or aspects of a situation that appear to be out of place or which may not fit with the rest of the plan. As a result, he has a high drive for change and may become impatient with others if they do not move as quickly to understand a concept or to act on a situation. This high drive will be seen in his sense of urgency.

Drive Results : Chris Sample

Drive/Influence is related to the ability of the candidate to be self-sufficient after the initial training period. This includes the ability to be bold and persuasive and to independently pursue answers to difficult customer challenges. This dimension is associated with a willingness to do for oneself what the company may find difficult to find time for (i.e. continuous training on product knowledge). Related to this measure is the courage and willingness to assume calculated risks in order to assist the customer.

This candidate indicates a strong desire to be independent. This dimension is important for success in business and management; however, he scores above the typical range. With his strong drive to be in control and promote his ideas, he may override the ideas and desires of others to a point that may actually alienate or offend. He may be able to accomplish a number of tasks and goals through the sheer force of his convictions and perseverance; however, without others being motivated on their own, it can be quite demanding and taxing on the organization. How well he is received as a manager will greatly depend on his level of self- awareness regarding his interpersonal skills, as well as his capacity to monitor and regulate his drive and emotion.

Sometimes, specific scores make larger contributions or detractions to the management role. In regard to Drive/Influence here are areas to consider:

A contributor to his score is his interest in being in control of his circumstances and of generally being in charge. This attribute is positive for most business circumstances where control of the business is ultimately his responsibility. This dimension includes taking active steps to confront others, being assertive, and readily managing situations as they arise.

A strong contributor to this score is his high degree of social boldness. This trait assists him in the process of venturing out, creating influence, and getting things done. His adventurous nature may not be disruptive, providing there are balancing traits that help him stay focused. Otherwise, he may tend to be more interested in the journey itself rather than in completing the matters at hand.

Stress Tolerance : Chris Sample

This score reflects the candidate's capacity to remain calm under pressure, initiate action, and manage frustration and disappointment. It is related to self assuredness, goal- directedness, and endurance through obstacles. Resilience is associated with managing oneself professionally with customers and co-workers alike even when in stressful circumstances.

This candidate reports himself as being more calm, confident, and composed than other successful managers. If this is an accurate self -assessment, it suggests that he is not easily upset. His resilience can be helpful in managing a business. It may also suggest that it is difficult for him to feel motivated to change if he is comfortable and unworried about his circumstances. In this regard, his comfort with himself may limit his ability to be open to suggestions for areas of development. In addition, he may not be interested in moving outside his comfortable zone of confidence. This high score may also indicate a certain degree of emotional stiffness or distance. It is important to know whether he is in touch with his emotions; it is also important for him to understand what is potentially upsetting or of concern to others.

Sometimes, specific scores make larger contributions or detractions to the management role. In regard to Stress Tolerance here are areas to consider:

He reports himself as being able to respond in a stable and emotionally consistent fashion. Life is basically on solid ground, and he is able to withstand most daily business challenges. Problems generally do not upset him, and he can recover quite rapidly from surprise and disappointment. His ability to recognize, understand, and control his emotions may have a positive impact on others’ abilities to relate easily with him, and others will find it easier to approach him. His calmness will allow him time to think through situations and will give him a greater chance to arrive at good solutions.

Execution Results : Chris Sample

Scores on this scale represent the candidate's capacity to direct her energies towards goals and commitments. This measure is related to attendance and consistency of behavior. Execution is associated with timeliness, focus, organization skills, and attention to detail. This trait is also related to the candidate’s interest in following the procedures and the guidelines of the organization and one’s preferred style of operation (i.e. systematic and orderly or flexible and spontaneous). Execution is a measure of how planful the candidate is relative to meeting company and customer needs; does the person follow through?

This candidate reports a slightly higher degree of Execution than is typical for managers. This score most likely represents a strong sense of conscientiousness and a self-disciplined approach to work. Although self- discipline is important in management, it is also advantageous to have some flexibility so that routines do not restrict his ability to be open to new approaches. In a technical environment, it is important to pay attention to the disciplines and procedures that are in place; however, in charting new directions, it will also be important for him to have the flexibility to look in other areas. It is also helpful to be able to adjust to different business situations and circumstances and to listen to the perspectives of others, including customers and employees.

Sometimes, specific scores make larger contributions or detractions to the management role. In regard to Execution here are areas to consider:

A strong contributor to his score is his expressed high interest in following guidelines. This trait is an important component for tending to the basic structure, boundaries, and policies of an organization. However, he indicates such a strong adherence to—almost a dependence on—rules, regulations, and routines that his style may interfere with his ability to respond flexibly to changes that are inherent in any business. In addition, the strict support of policies and procedures may not allow others the flexibility needed to respond to changing situations in a timely manner. His strong sense of boundaries may interfere with his ability to appreciate other perspectives.

Interpersonal Style Results : Chris Sample

This scale measures the candidate’s social and interpersonal skills. It emphasizes how well the person expresses oneself and the willingness and comfort in talking with customers and co-workers. This includes how the candidate would present to the customer, how effectively this person would express support for customer concerns, and how proficient the person is in listening to others. Scores in the Parker Range suggest that the candidate is likely to be warm, energetic, reasonably bold, polite and interested in working within a team environments.

He falls within the typical range as compared to other managers, indicating that he is outgoing and people- oriented. This score is a composite score made up of several individual characteristics. Generally, he reports an awareness and interest in activities and issues that involve personal interaction with people. The advantages of being people- oriented lie in the practical fact that the activities of managers are in large part involved with people and relationships. Therefore, being naturally attentive to those issues has advantages.

Sometimes, specific scores make larger contributions or detractions to the management role. In regard to Interpersonal Style here are areas to consider:

He reports a high interest in being adventurous and in initiating contact in a wide variety of social situations. His approach can be helpful, particularly in management roles that require travel and frequent change. However, he is reporting such extreme social boldness that he may not remain satisfied with events for long. He enjoys the spotlight, and he may crave attention. Although meeting and interacting with new people may be part of the fun and adventure of a job, it may also take him away from attending to the routine or procedural areas of the job. The limiting characteristic of this more extreme score will be a tendency to become overly involved and stretched in too many areas at once.

Innovation & Change Orientation Results : Chris Sample

This dimension focuses on sensitivity to people and to ideas. It measures the willingness to be open to and to try new approaches to get to the customer. Balance on this dimension suggests responsiveness to how others feel and think while being practical, factual, and business-minded. Intuition can assist the candidate in “reading” the customer and responding appropriately to his or her needs. It is a measure of sensitivity to others.

This candidate’s overall score on the Innovation & Change Orientation dimension suggests a more sensitive, receptive, and subjective approach to relating to the world. This style can be helpful in the service/sales side of the business or in any area that calls for more people interaction. However, he may take events and disappointments more to heart and be more prone to internalize conflict and disagreement. You may want to consider how well he works with facts and figures. Additionally, you will need to determine if he has enough practical focus and resilience to address the challenges that are inherent in managing large groups of individuals. For instance, he may hesitate to enter into conflict situations where feelings may get hurt. As such, it will be important for him to learn how to manage conflict in a productive manner. It will also be important for him to not shy away from situations that require pushing for change or dealing with progress.

Sometimes, specific scores make larger contributions or detractions to the management role. In regard to Innovation & Change Orientation here are areas to consider:

His desire for change diminishes his score. His drive for change is so high that he may push the bounds of reality. He has a high need for progress and development, and his interest in experimenting and moving quickly is high. He may find that colleagues, subordinates, and events do not move as quickly as he thinks they should; therefore, he may be impatient or critical at times. He may also direct these expectations inward and become frustrated with his perceived lack of progress or success. How this style is accepted is related to the culture of the organization. More aggressive and progressive cultures will accept his style more easily than more conservative ones.

Interpreting The Results

All information in this report is confidential and should be treated responsibly.

This report is based on computerized comparisons between individual responses and the professional norm on the 16pf® Questionnaire. It is prone to various sources of error and should be interpreted with caution. This report is intended to assist in the interview process, not to substitute for it or for other data. The candidate's scores for each scale are discussed on individual pages.

BEFORE YOU INTERVIEW

This report will tell you how well your candidate fits with the foloowing personality characteristics that help predict success in professionals:

  • Intellectual Capability
  • Drive / Influence
  • Stress Tolerance
  • Execution
  • Interpersonal Style
  • Innovation & Change Orientation

Note where your candidate stands on these characteristics and then consider the suggested follow-up interview questions and accompanying “listen for” statements. Keep in mind that while it is better to be higher than lower on these dimensions, sometimes a candidate can have “too much of a good thing.” Don’t be afraid to supplement the questions provided with some of your own.

AFTER YOU INTERVIEW

To make a final decision on a candidate, consider what you learned from this report and the follow-up interview, along with other factors like experience, education, references, etc. Match what you have learned to what is important in the job and then make a hiring decision that you can explain in an objective manner. Be ready to answer this question: Why is this person a good fit - or not - for the job?

If you hire this person, be sure to come back to this assessment afterwards to think about what the individual needs to do in order grow professionally and be successful in their role. Use this information as a management tool and as the start of their individual development plan that you put together as their HR representative or manager.
