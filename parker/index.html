Technical Competence : Chris Sample

Technical competence refers to the person's ability to perform the technical functions of Field Engineering as it relates to math, verbal and general reasoning.

Technical Competence : Math

This measures the person's ability to solve math problems that include basic math, as well as algebra and geometry.

Higher numerical reasoning supports the computational aspects of Field Engineering project work. Those with higher scores will typically gain credibility for their mathematical competence and ability to extend various technical conversations.

Lower numerical reasoning can hinder the field support work and internal computational aspects of managing client projects and completing various audits, studies and business development activities. One's credibility can be compromised if in-the-moment mathematical computation is required to extend exploratory conversations. Credibility can also be restricted in failing to complete certain internal Field Engineering functions.

Technical Competence : Verbal

This measures the person's ability to understand and utilize written language through the use of grammar, analogies, and comprehension.

Higher scores tend to communicate in an articulate manner given an overall healthy verbal ability. Higher scores will share technical and non technical engineering information in a manner others can easily understand.

Lower scores can find it challenging to convey complex conceptual information. Lower verbal skills can impact the way Field Engineers share technical customer information as well restrict their effectiveness communicating with other distributor and FE resources.

Technical Competence : Reasoning

This measures the person's abilty to understand and analyze information that is presented visually and solve problems using logic.

Higher scores indicate the ability to recognize underlying patterns and use deductive reasoning to determine the ways in which informations is being presented. People like this tend to apply a more creative or flexible in their thinking which can be particularly helpful in their efforts to work with customers.

Lower scores suggest that the person's ability may be limited when faced with problems that require a more logical approach that involves pattern recognition and deductive reasoning. People who score like this tend to stick to decision rules that have worked for them in the past, regardless of their appropriateness for the problem at hand, and can have difficulty coming up with more creative approaches.

Derailers : Chris Sample

The Derailers section reflects the uniqueness of the Field Engineer role. The combination of teamwork, intellect, persistence, social skills, and field exploration mixed with various job demands makes the role particularly special. As a result, there are a few critical aspects of behavior that can undermine successful performance.

Derailer : Pessimism

This scale measures the degree to which a Field Engineer may react to change, surprise or ideas that are not part of an original plan. This could accompany cynicism and a reluctance to embrace change and an unwillingness to explore possibilities limiting customer conversation and restricting willingness to extend collaboration efforts.

Higher scores have a pronounced reflex to respond to change, surprise and exploration in a negative way. Those higher on this scale can find it challenging to trust themselves and others when veering off of a pre-determined script when exploring ideas in the office and in front of customers.

Lower scores tend to embrace change, surprise and exploratory conversation in a trusting, flexible manner. Higher scorers typically trust themselves and peers when plans change and things become ambiguous.

Derailer : Overprep

Some preparation is necessary to research customers, anticipate objections, and resourcefully challenge assumptions. However, too much preparation can restrict the flexibility to initiate dialogue and experiment with various technical solutions. This scale measures the degree of preparation one prefers when engaging in various types of Field Engineering work.

Higher scores often find it necessary to thoroughly prepare ahead of time. They often gain confidence in their preparation and find it difficult in placing practical limits on having enough data before initiating action. In conversation, they can revert to over-talking and sharing too much of the intricacies of an engineering solution, restricting their customer focus to solving a broader need.

Lower scores tend to be more fluid and dynamic in their preparation ahead of various customer engagements and engineering projects. They will not tend to become flustered when faced with ambiguity, interruptions and customer questions.

Derailer : Critical

This scale measures the degree of criticality expressed. Being particular and finicky about certain details often accompanies progressiveness and high standards -aspects that many successful Field Engineers possess. However, the limits of being too critical can undermine collaboration and teamwork necessary to meet challenging customer requests.

Higher scores often are rather progressive and energized by exploring new and unchartered territory. Often accompanying their thirst for newness is a highly refined level of detail. Some can describe high scorers as picky, finicky and difficult to please.

Lower scores can be more forgiving with some of the details not being perfect. They can be more fluid and okay when working collaboratively with peers within the distributorship as their standards will tend to not be overly inflated.

Derailer : Expert

Many Field Engineers are viewed as a local subject matter expert and are relied upon for the technical skillset and access to a broader community that can provide sound insight in helping solve substantial customer challenges. This scale measures the sense of importance one gains when being relied upon for a particular knowledge, skill, and experience.

Higher scores tend to relish the prominence that goes along with being relied upon in a technical setting. Their sense of importance is reinforced by their engineering acumen. If this is not accompanied by good humility and awareness to place team accomplishment over individual accolades, they can be ineffective. If taken to an extreme, their craving for recognition and the need to chase a particular trophy can outweigh the necessity to honor existing systems and teamwork.

Lower scores tend to not seek the limelight and are more comfortable promoting team over self accomplishment. They can enjoy working collaboratively and may over defer to others when showing stronger personal conviction could be necessary. In particular, a certain level of ego and confidence is typically required to comfortably step in as an expert. If underdone, the Field Engineer can fail to speak with conviction and authority in key situations.

Role Alignment

The Role Alignment section reflects how in tune a candidate's work interests are to that of a Field Engineer. For those whose interests align well with this job, they prefer to spend a fair amount of time in the field helping customers solve problems, as well as conducting audits, providing training and presenting product information. At the same time they also show interest in researching ideas, analyzing data, and following up on various projects or work completed within the distributorship.

Higher scores enjoy work that includes a fair amount of a hands-on, resourcefulness that yields practical results supporting the bottom line goals of the business.

Lower scores may not enjoy the field aspects of helping customers solve real-world technical problems research over venturing out and spending time with various customers.

Interpreting The Results

All information in this report is confidential and should be treated responsibly.

This report is based on computerized comparisons between individual responses and the field engineering norm on the Watterson Personality Inventory. It is prone to various sources of error and should be interpreted with caution. This report is intended to assist in the interview process, not to substitute for it or for other data. The candidate's scores for each scale are discussed on individual pages.

BEFORE YOU INTERVIEW

This report will tell you how well your candidate fits with the characteristics that help predict success in Field Engineers:

Note where your candidate stands on these characteristics and then consider the suggested follow-up interview questions and accompanying “listen for” statements. Keep in mind that (with the exception of the derailers) while it is better to be higher than lower on these dimensions, sometimes a candidate can have “too much of a good thing.” Don’t be afraid to supplement the questions provided with some of your own.

AFTER YOU INTERVIEW

In order to make a final decision on a candidate, consider what you learned from this report and the follow-up interview, along with other factors like experience, education, references, etc. Match what you have learned to what is important in the job and then make a hiring decision that you can explain in an objective manner. Be ready to answer this question: Why is this person a good fit - or not - for the job?

If you hire this person, be sure to come back to this assessment afterwards to think about what the individual needs to do in order grow professionally and be successful as a Field Engineer. Use this information as a management tool and as the start of their individual development plan that you put together as their manager.
