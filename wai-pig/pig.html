Drive / Influence Results : Chris Sample

Drive/Influence is related to the ability of the candidate to exhibit persuasive, assertive, and influential behaviors. This includes the ability to be bold and persuasive and to pursue answers to difficult challenges. Related to this measure is the courage and willingness to assume calculated risks to achieve desired results. There often is a certain amount of vigilance to assure desired objectives. This dimension assesses the drive to challenge others, resolve issues, and move with urgency for change and perceived progress. Many of these behaviors are not a core part of being a professional in a role with no direct reports; however, in certain roles, there is an advantage to have some of them. Determine which behaviors are critical and see how these fit with this score. Also, if this individual is seen as a future candidate for a management/leader role, look to have this score be a little higher than the average here.

The scores for Sample fall within the target range when compared to strong professionals. This suggests that he likes progress and typically takes active steps to achieve it. His ability to take an independent stand in the midst of an organization and his willingness to manage these efforts seem to be in a good range. How well he is received as a professional will greatly depend on his level of self-awareness regarding his interpersonal skills, as well as his capacity to monitor and regulate his drive and emotion.

Modifying his Drive/Influence score is his preference to be accommodating. Professionals typically report a higher interest in being in charge and in controlling business circumstances. He may use company processes or a disciplined approach to take control, versus being more personally assertive. This approach may be effective in a structured system; however, in more flexible and fluid business situations, it is important to exercise more personal authority.

Self-Control Results : Chris Sample

Scores on this scale represent the candidate's capacity to direct her focus and discipline towards goals and commitments. Self-Control is associated with timeliness, focus, organization skills, and consistency of behavior. It is highly representative of a conscientious person driven to meet expectations. This might include high attention to details, accuracy, and precision, as well as, following processes and procedures. This dimension is a little higher than for managers/leaders as the activities are usually of a more task focused nature.

Sample falls within the target range of other successful professionals. Balance is highly important in this personality dimension. He indicates an overall respect for systems and procedures; however, he is not so bound to systems and procedures that he cannot circumvent them when necessary. Management requires some flexibility, as well as a capacity to not get mired in the details of day-to-day operations if he aspires to manage others.

He expresses interest in following guidelines and being conscientious.. This orientation to work is important for tending to the basic structure, processes, and policies of an organization. However, he indicates such a strong adherence to rules, regulations, and routines that his style may interfere with his ability to respond flexibly and timely to changes that are inherent in any business.

Interpersonal Style Results : Chris Sample

This scale measures the candidate’s social and interpersonal skills. It emphasizes how well the person expresses oneself and the willingness and comfort in talking with others. This includes how the candidate would present to an audience, how effectively this person would express support for others’ concerns, and how proficient the person is in listening. Good interpersonal skills positively contribute to collaboration and strong internal and external relationships. For a more technical professional, this may not be as important a characteristic as it would for a manager/leader; thus, the average score is more in the middle.

Sample scores in the target range for top performing professionals indicating that he is outgoing and people-oriented. This score is a composite score made up of several individual characteristics. Generally, he reports an awareness and interest in activities and issues that involve personal interaction with people. The advantages of being people-oriented lie in the practical fact that the activities of professionals are in large part involved with people and relationships. Therefore, being naturally attentive to those issues has advantages.

He has higher interest in people, and he is able to show warmth and empathy for others. Typically, his relationships with others will tend to be collaborative. He is most likely able to show a sincere concern for the well-being of others, which will be helpful in his efforts to be part of the team. Potentially, his desire to please others may limit his impact as a professional if he allows it to get in the way of his own goals and responsibilities.

Thinking Styles : Chris Sample

These are four conceptual thinking styles relevant to work. They are based on an individual’s own statements about how they think. This is more about style than ability. It is our experience that people tend to overestimate their ability to think and learn. Particularly, make note of the score balance of Logical and Creativity/Innovation. Generally, a balance is preferred for most productive thought, often characterized as good judgment. Scores on these scales range from 2 to 9.

Thinking Styles : Logical

This scale reflects a person’s emphasis on using facts and logic to approach life choices. Persons high on this scale approach life keeping the big picture in view and solve problems with a systematic process. Persons lower on this scale are less structured in their approach and typically place more emphasis on feeling, emotion, and sentiment. As a thinking style, this is more relevant to the standard work expected in industries or parts of a business with a high component of processes, procedures and systems. This is typically in production, manufacturing, and administration enterprises where execution is the focus. Sample questions include: “Before I act, I consider the pros and cons” and “I go at things with logic regardless of my feelings.”

Thinking Styles : Creativity / Innovation

This scale speaks to a person’s preference for new ideas, different approaches, exploring, and creating. Those higher on this dimension have an urgency for change; like to experiment; are detailed about improvements but not routine. They are more comfortable with ambiguity and uncertainty. People lower like clear plans, specific instructions, tradition, and predictability. Within many professional roles, there are times to be practical, and times that call for a more unconventional solution to a common problem. Sample questions include: “I see new things to try before others do” and “I don’t manage lack of uncertainty very well.”

Thinking Styles : Detail

This scale focuses on a person’s attention to detail and their capacity to remember the little things around them. People higher on this scale have detailed memories, are quick to spot little things out of place, and are quick to record these details. People lower on this scale are less mindful of details around them and are less likely to recall specifics, facts, and details. Sample questions include: “I notice details that others don’t” and People say that I have a memory that never fails.”

Thinking Styles : Learning

This scale highlights aspects of “Learning Agility.” Speed and interest in learning is captured. People higher on this dimension focus on enjoyment in the challenge of learning new information, discovery, and the constant pursuit of gaining skills and knowledge to productively apply them in new or novel situations. People lower on this scale are less driven to pursue new learning as compared to being comfortable with their current level of acquired knowledge and understanding.

Generally higher scores are positively related to success; however, there is not as much emphasis on this dimension where there is a high demand for standard work with a high value on following processes and procedures. Here, execution of known information in a logical manner is of paramount value. If, however, bringing new ideas into a part of the business is critical, having a good balance of new learning with an appreciation for the existing processes is important. Sample questions include: “I keep up to date with all the new ideas” and I like to understand why something is the way it is.”

Interpreting The Results

All information in this report is confidential and should be treated responsibly.

This report is based on computerized comparisons between individual responses and the professional norm on the Watterson Personality Inventory plus additional ability measures. It is prone to various sources of error and should be interpreted with caution. This report is intended to assist in the interview process, not to substitute for it or for other data. The candidate's scores for each scale are discussed on individual pages.

BEFORE YOU INTERVIEW

This report will tell you how well your candidate fits with the “Big 5” personality characteristics that help predict success:

  • Drive/Influence
  • Emotional Resilience
  • Self-Control
  • Interpersonal Style
  • Practicality

Ability measures presented are based on the type of job and may include Intellectual Strength, General Knowledge, and/or Mechanical Ability.

Note where your candidate stands on these characteristics and then consider the suggested follow-up interview questions and accompanying “listen for” statements. Keep in mind that while it is better to be higher than lower on these dimensions, sometimes a candidate can have “too much of a good thing.” Don’t be afraid to supplement the questions provided with some of your own.

AFTER YOU INTERVIEW

To make a final decision on a candidate, consider what you learned from this report and the follow-up interview, along with other factors like experience, education, references, etc. Match what you have learned to what is important in the job and then make a hiring decision that you can explain in an objective manner. Be ready to answer this question: Why is this person a good fit - or not - for the job?

If you hire this person, be sure to come back to this assessment afterwards to think about what the individual needs to do in order grow professionally and be successful in their role. Use this information as a management tool and as the start of their individual development plan that you put together as their HR representative or manager.
