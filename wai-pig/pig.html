Thinking Styles : Chris Sample

These are four conceptual thinking styles relevant to work. They are based on an individual’s own statements about how they think. This is more about style than ability. It is our experience that people tend to overestimate their ability to think and learn. Particularly, make note of the score balance of Logical and Creativity/Innovation. Generally, a balance is preferred for most productive thought, often characterized as good judgment. Scores on these scales range from 2 to 9.

Thinking Styles : Logical

This scale reflects a person’s emphasis on using facts and logic to approach life choices. Persons high on this scale approach life keeping the big picture in view and solve problems with a systematic process. Persons lower on this scale are less structured in their approach and typically place more emphasis on feeling, emotion, and sentiment. As a thinking style, this is more relevant to the standard work expected in industries or parts of a business with a high component of processes, procedures and systems. This is typically in production, manufacturing, and administration enterprises where execution is the focus. Sample questions include: “Before I act, I consider the pros and cons” and “I go at things with logic regardless of my feelings.”

Thinking Styles : Creativity / Innovation

This scale speaks to a person’s preference for new ideas, different approaches, exploring, and creating. Those higher on this dimension have an urgency for change; like to experiment; are detailed about improvements but not routine. They are more comfortable with ambiguity and uncertainty. People lower like clear plans, specific instructions, tradition, and predictability. Within many professional roles, there are times to be practical, and times that call for a more unconventional solution to a common problem. Sample questions include: “I see new things to try before others do” and “I don’t manage lack of uncertainty very well.”

Thinking Styles : Detail

This scale focuses on a person’s attention to detail and their capacity to remember the little things around them. People higher on this scale have detailed memories, are quick to spot little things out of place, and are quick to record these details. People lower on this scale are less mindful of details around them and are less likely to recall specifics, facts, and details. Sample questions include: “I notice details that others don’t” and People say that I have a memory that never fails.”

Thinking Styles : Learning

This scale highlights aspects of “Learning Agility.” Speed and interest in learning is captured. People higher on this dimension focus on enjoyment in the challenge of learning new information, discovery, and the constant pursuit of gaining skills and knowledge to productively apply them in new or novel situations. People lower on this scale are less driven to pursue new learning as compared to being comfortable with their current level of acquired knowledge and understanding.

Generally higher scores are positively related to success; however, there is not as much emphasis on this dimension where there is a high demand for standard work with a high value on following processes and procedures. Here, execution of known information in a logical manner is of paramount value. If, however, bringing new ideas into a part of the business is critical, having a good balance of new learning with an appreciation for the existing processes is important. Sample questions include: “I keep up to date with all the new ideas” and I like to understand why something is the way it is.”

Ability Measures : Intellectual Strength

Scores on this scale reflect the candidate's natural ability to solve problems of an abstract nature. Because these problems are language free and rely on simple figures, the ability to solve these questions/puzzles tends to be uninfluenced by exposure to formal education. Thus, candidates who may not have had much schooling may have score poorly on General Knowledge questions, and yet, may score quite well on this dimension.

Chris Sample scores higher on this dimension as compared to other successful professionals. This capacity to reason through and imagine solutions is critical to success.

Ability Measures : General Knowledge

Scores on this scale reflect the applicant’s General Ability to solve common verbal and mathematical problems typically learned in high school. There is a technical, applied aspect to these problems so that a review of the high school subject matter may help if scores are lower than expected. These scores are based on what a person has learned either from formal schooling or life experience. Low scores may indicate difficulty in solving common problems and a lower level of skill in material usually learned in high school. Inquiring into school and formal learning may be useful here. Be alert for low study skills, poor discipline and/or low school motivation.

Chris Sample most likely has applied himself diligently in school and other learning environments which results in a high score on this measure. He is very capable when working with vocabulary, mathematical and abstract learning which will help him to quickly learn new concepts and ideas, understand complex systems and processes, and communicate his thoughts to others.

Ability Measures : Mechanical Comprehension

Scores on this scale are related to general thinking skills; however, they concentrate more on the practical, mechanical elements and physical forces. Scores can be influenced by the amount of real life exposure to these elements. It might be helpful to explore past exposure to mechanical issues.

Chris Sample possesses a strong ability to work with mechanical concepts and devices. This ability will help him to quickly learn and appreciate the information related to technology, particularly as it relates to the physical world. Depending on his educational background, he may have extensive, specific knowledge within a particular technical area. As such, he is comfortable working in and around these types of environments.

Interpreting The Results

All information in this report is confidential and should be treated responsibly.

This report is based on computerized comparisons between individual responses and the field engineering norm on the Watterson Personality Inventory. It is prone to various sources of error and should be interpreted with caution. This report is intended to assist in the interview process, not to substitute for it or for other data. The candidate's scores for each scale are discussed on individual pages.

BEFORE YOU INTERVIEW

This report will tell you how well your candidate fits with the five ARISE characteristics that help predict success in Field Engineers:

  • Agility
  • Resilient
  • Intentional
  • Self-Starting
  • Engaging

Note where your candidate stands on these characteristics and then consider the suggested follow-up interview questions and accompanying “listen for” statements. Keep in mind that (with the exception of the derailers) while it is better to be higher than lower on these dimensions, sometimes a candidate can have “too much of a good thing.” Don’t be afraid to supplement the questions provided with some of your own.

AFTER YOU INTERVIEW

In order to make a final decision on a candidate, consider what you learned from this report and the follow-up interview, along with other factors like experience, education, references, etc. Match what you have learned to what is important in the job and then make a hiring decision that you can explain in an objective manner. Be ready to answer this question: Why is this person a good fit - or not - for the job?

If you hire this person, be sure to come back to this assessment afterwards to think about what the individual needs to do in order grow professionally and be successful as a Field Engineer. Use this information as a management tool and as the start of their individual development plan that you put together as their manager.
